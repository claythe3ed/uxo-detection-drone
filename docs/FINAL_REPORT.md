Technical Report: UXO Detection Drone System

This project designs and simulates a low-cost, open-source unmanned aerial vehicle for the detection of landmines and unexploded ordnance using a fluxgate magnetometer and a thermal infrared camera. The work builds upon the Maggy platform published in IEEE Access in 2024 and extends it with a ten-object target library, real environmental data, and advanced physics simulations.

Methodology. Scientific literature was collected via custom web spiders targeting arXiv, GitHub, DBLP, RSS feeds from engineering blogs, and Zenodo. Three key papers were downloaded and converted to text to extract equations and parameters. For environmental data, a region in Bosnia was selected as representative of a former conflict zone, at coordinates 44 degrees north and 18 degrees east. Hourly weather data for January 2024, including air temperature and soil temperature at depths of 0 to 7 centimeters and 7 to 28 centimeters, were retrieved from the Open-Meteo API. The elevation of the point, 649 meters, was obtained from the Open-Meteo Elevation API. Soil properties were extracted from SoilGrids version 2.0 at a depth of 0 to 5 centimeters, yielding 29 percent clay, 36 percent sand, and 81.5 percent soil organic carbon. The thermal diffusivity of the background soil was computed as approximately 1.08 times ten to the negative seven square meters per second.

The drone airframe and electronics were designed using the Blueprints.am platform, driven by a prompt derived from the Maggy paper parameters. The design output includes a bill of materials with an estimated total cost of 1,405 US dollars, excluding the thermal camera; electrical and mechanical connection files in JSON format; an assembly guide; a configuration file; and a visual rendering.

A target library was assembled containing ten objects commonly found in post-conflict environments. These include anti-personnel mines such as PMN-2, M16 AP, VS-50, and PMN-4; anti-tank mines such as TM-62M and M15; a hand grenade (F1); 60-millimeter and 82-millimeter mortar rounds; and a 500-pound Mk82 bomb. For each target, the magnetic moment and thermal properties were recorded. Magnetic moments range from 0.1 ampere square meters for small plastic mines to 120 ampere square meters for the Mk82.

Simulations. Magnetic detection was simulated using a magnetic dipole model. The flight altitude was set to 1 meter and the target burial depth to 0.3 meters. The magnetometer sensitivity, 13 nanotesla per least significant bit, sets a detection threshold of 0.494 microtesla. The peak magnetic field is calculated as ten to the negative seven times the magnetic moment divided by the cube of the distance. Thermal detection was simulated by solving the one-dimensional unsteady Fourier heat conduction equation with the Crank-Nicolson finite difference scheme. The boundary conditions applied a sinusoidal surface temperature representing a diurnal cycle and a constant temperature at a deep boundary. The model compared a homogeneous soil background against a scenario with a buried object of different thermal diffusivity, and the thermal contrast was computed as the difference between the surface temperature above the object and the surface temperature above undisturbed soil.

Results. Magnetically, only large steel objects such as the TM-62M, M15, and Mk82 produced fields exceeding the detection threshold. Small plastic mines and small metal fragments generated fields below 0.1 microtesla and cannot be reliably detected by the magnetometer alone. Thermally, all ten targets were detectable at 0.3 meters depth. Plastic mines exhibited a positive thermal contrast reaching up to 4.6 degrees Celsius during midday hours. Steel objects exhibited a negative contrast reaching down to negative 2.5 degrees Celsius during the late night. A thermal camera with a noise equivalent temperature difference of less than 50 millikelvin, such as the FLIR Boson, is recommended for field deployment. The optimal detection windows diverge: plastic mines are best observed between 12:00 and 15:00 local time, while steel objects are best observed between 02:00 and 05:00 local time. A dual-sortie survey, one daytime and one nighttime, can cover all target types with high probability.

Repository contents. The repository includes a bilingual README file describing the humanitarian purpose of the project, a timestamped master knowledge graph tracking all sources, three Python simulation scripts, weather and terrain and soil data in JSON format, a CSV file of simulation results, four generated images, the ten-object target library in JSON, the Blueprints.am design files including the bill of materials and assembly guide, the original PDFs of the key scientific papers, and a cost breakdown of the bill of materials.

Limitations and future work. The simulation assumes a homogeneous soil background, while real fields contain vegetation, rocks, and dynamic moisture. The thermal model is one-dimensional; a two- or three-dimensional extension would improve the accuracy of lateral heat spread. Magnetometer interference from the drone's own electronics was partially mitigated by a retractable boom but was not fully modeled. Field validation with an assembled prototype is necessary.

Conclusion. The proposed dual-sensor drone system is capable of detecting all tested landmine and unexploded ordnance types at a depth of 0.3 meters, with an affordable bill of materials estimated below 1,700 US dollars including a suitable thermal camera. The open-source release under the MIT and Apache 2.0 licenses is intended to support humanitarian demining efforts worldwide.
